As we have personally discussed with most Tera investors already, Tera has been slowly but steadily reducing its exposure to Russia since January of this year.

This is not because our fundamental views of Russia have changed. They remain very much in effect. However, over the past 18 months we have reached the conclusion that, unless Russia is rerated by global investors, Russian public equity markets will continue to simply move sidewise (at best). In other words, the extremely low P/E ratios of Russian companies are not due to the fundamentals of those companies (as we have consistently maintained), but to the perceptions of Russia by mainly Western investors.

This being the case, and since Vladimir Putin remains the symbol of Russia to most people outside of its borders, this means that, for so long as he remains the Russian president, Russian markets will not be rerated, its P/E ratios will remain very low and the public equity investment climate will remain poor.

As Putin will remain in office for at least four more years, we decided late last year to begin reducing Tera's exposure to Russia to zero.

To the left, you will see the results of that decision: the RTS has steadily declined about 5% a month since January. We submitted our first redemption request to exit an underlying fund (HH Generation, in red) in December, that fund hit a multi-year peak in January when Tera exited, and has fallen rapidly and steadily since. (We have since submitted redemption requests for all of the funds in which Tera was invested.)

The largest fund in Russia, Prosperity Capital, in which Tera has occasionally invested over the years, has drawn a descending line parallel to the RTS in terms of performance, although it began tracing that line one month later.

While this provides Tera with a 10% YTD outperformance compared to the RTS, 15% compared to HH Generation and 8% compared to Prosperity, Tera has not yet exited Russia completely and is down 3.5% YTD through May. (Tera's June NAV was not available when this letter was prepared.)

With the exception of the "Bad News" described below, Tera will have fully exited Russia once the June NAV for the remaining Russian fund is issued and the proceeds from the final redemption received.

The Future
Tera will remain a fund of funds, but it will henceforth only invest in funds which are traded on the Exante fund platform (https://exante.eu/funds/) on which Tera was listed in the middle of last year. An equally weighted fund index based on 20 of these funds was up 27% last year and has been up at least 15% every year since the platform was created. By selecting the best funds on the platform (and definitely fewer than 20), Tera hopes to exceed even this performance and provide significantly greater returns to its shareholders - and far greater returns than would be provided by continuing to invest in Russia-focused funds).

This redeployment will be completed by the end of August and normal investor communication through fact sheets, website updates and other means will resume at that time.

Since we were able to manage Tera to inclusion among the top 10 funds of funds in the world four times over the past seven years (No. 1, 2, 8 and 9), we are confident that we will do even better in an environment with far broader possibilities.
